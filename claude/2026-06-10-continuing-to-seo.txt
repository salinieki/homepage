❯ Can you suggest alternative formulations for "keeping alive"                  

⏺ A few options that drop cleanly into the same slot ("…based in London, X in
  the United Kingdom since 2012."):

  - carrying on the tradition of Latvian folk dance — closest in tone;
  emphasises continuity without sounding endangered.
  - preserving the tradition of Latvian folk dance — more formal; good for SEO
  (preserve + tradition is a strong pairing).
  - upholding the tradition of Latvian folk dance — slight sense of
  duty/heritage; fits diaspora framing.
  - continuing the tradition of Latvian folk dance — neutral, plain; no
  implication that it's at risk.
  - nurturing the tradition of Latvian folk dance — warmer; suggests active
  growth rather than rescue.
  - championing Latvian folk dance — more outward/promotional; drops "tradition"
   so reads punchier.

  If you want it to sound less like the group is on a rescue mission and more
  like a living, growing community, carrying on or nurturing read best.
  Preserving is the safest SEO play because "Latvian folk dance tradition" is
  the keyword cluster Google clusters around.

❯ What do you mean by NewsArticle JSON-LD on the news/ pages?                   
⏺ NewsArticle (or Article / BlogPosting) is a schema.org JSON-LD type you put in
   <head> on each news page to tell Google "this is a dated article with an
  author and a headline." It gives search results:

  - Date stamps ("Posted Jul 27, 2025") shown next to the snippet.
  - Author name in the result.
  - Image thumbnail alongside the result (only with valid image + proper
  dimensions).
  - Eligibility for the Top Stories / Discover surfaces — those are mostly for
  journalism publishers in practice, but the markup is the same.

  For a concert recap or season update, Article or BlogPosting is more accurate
  than NewsArticle (which is really for journalism). Behaviour in Google is
  effectively the same; just pick the truthful type.

  The block would live in a head.html partial that runs on single news pages,
  populated from frontmatter you already have — title, date, author, coverImage,
   summary. Roughly:

❯ How much impact does it have that the english pages use latvian URLs? And how 
   much effort would it be to change that?                                      

⏺ Impact: modest.

  For pure SEO ranking, URL slugs are a weak signal — Google decides what a page
   is about from content, title, headings, and links far more than from the URL.
   So changing /en/par-mums/ → /en/about/ won't move you up the search results
  meaningfully.

  Where it does help:
  - Credibility on share: when someone pastes /en/par-mums/ into a
  Slack/WhatsApp/LinkedIn link preview, the URL undermines the "this is the
  English version" signal. /en/about/ reads as legitimately English.
  - CTR from search: Google sometimes shows the URL breadcrumb under results.
  English slug = one more keyword match for an English-speaking searcher's
  query.
  - A small image-search / anchor-text boost — links shared with descriptive
  anchors do better.

  Overall it's a polish item, not a ranking unlock.

  Effort: small, ~30–60 minutes. The Hugo-specific pieces:

  1. Per-page URL override in each .en.md frontmatter:
  url: "/en/about/"
  1. This is the cleanest path. It doesn't break Hugo's translation-pairing
  (which links by base filename, so the files stay as par-mums.en.md etc.), and
  .Permalink / hreflang / canonical all use the override automatically.
  2. Update hugo.toml — the four English menu URLs.
  3. Grep for hardcoded paths — quick sweep through content/, layouts/, static/
  for any literal /en/par-mums/, /en/foto/, etc.
  4. 301 redirects from the old URLs — important if anything is already indexed.
   Hugo doesn't emit redirects natively; this depends on your host (Netlify
  _redirects, Cloudflare Pages _redirects, Vercel, or a meta refresh page).
  Without redirects, any current Google ranking on the old URL evaporates.

  So: real work is steps 1–3 (small), and step 4 is conditional on where you
  deploy. Want me to do 1–3 and tell you what the redirect file would look like
  for whichever host you use?

❯ What did you mean by "Favicon variants"?                                      

⏺ Right now baseof.html:66 declares just one favicon:

  <link rel="icon" type="image/png" href="{{ "images/logo.png" | relURL }}">

  That's enough for a browser tab, but it skips four other contexts:

  1. apple-touch-icon (180×180) — what iOS uses when someone taps "Add to Home
  Screen." Without it, the iPhone home-screen icon is a generic gray screenshot
  of the page instead of your logo.
  2. favicon.ico — legacy fallback at /favicon.ico that some browsers, RSS
  readers, and crawlers request directly regardless of what the HTML declares.
  3. Smaller PNG variants (16×16, 32×32) — sharper rendering in browser tabs at
  low DPI; the current 512+px logo gets downscaled poorly.
  4. Web app manifest + Android icons (192×192, 512×512) — needed for Android
  Chrome's "Add to Home Screen" to install with your icon, name, and theme
  colour rather than a generic fallback. Also a Lighthouse PWA criterion.

  Effort: ~20 minutes. Two reasonable paths:

  - Hugo-native — use resources.Get "images/logo.png" and .Resize to generate
  all variants at build time from the existing logo. Plus a small
  site.webmanifest template. No new assets in the repo.
  - External generator — drop images/logo.png into realfavicongenerator.net,
  download a zip, copy the files into static/, paste their <link> snippet into
  baseof.html. Less elegant, more bytes in the repo, but turnkey.

  The Hugo-native route is the same effort and keeps the repo clean. Want me to
  do that?
